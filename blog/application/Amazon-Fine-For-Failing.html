Amazon: $500,000 Fine for Failing to Notify California Workers About COVID-19 Cases

Amazon has been ordered to pay a fine of $500,000 for hiding the number of COVID-19 cases at its California workplaces from employees. First reported by the Los Angeles Times, the company agreed to pay the fine and improve the way it tracks cases and notifies workers and local health agencies. Amazon must also inform its warehouse workers in California of the “exact number of new COVID-19 cases in their workplaces” when cases arise.


Section 1:Background
The company has been under fire for its use of the H1B visa program. From The Sacramento Bee:

Amazon has come under fire in the past for being able to bring in foreign tech workers on the H1-B visa program to work in its warehouses. The visa allows U.S. companies to hire foreign workers for jobs in occupations that generally require specialised skills and a bachelor's degree or higher.

In February 2017, Amazon admitted that it used H1-B visas to hire foreign workers instead of native workers.
Amazon refused to reveal the number of H1-B-visas used at its warehouses and distribution centers in California in its annual H1-B filing with the Department of Labor.


COVID-19 Cases At Amazon Warehouses
COVID-19 is a lung disease caused by inhalation of silica dust. Between 2013 and 2017, Amazon did not report dozens of cases of COVID-19 to local public health agencies in California. In a statement, one California investigator said that “there is no evidence that Amazon’s failure to report any cases at all has contributed to the spread of this serious disease or made workers less safe.”

$500,000 is the maximum fine allowed by the California Environmental Protection Agency. The largest penalty issued in the state of California in the last five years was for $1 million to Dow Corning for failing to report silica dust-related cases of silicosis. Amazon must pay the fine within 45 days.


How Amazon Failed To Notify Workers And Health Agencies
The agreement between the California Department of Public Health and Amazon focuses on the number of COVID cases at the company's San Bernardino warehouse. Workers claim they had to repeatedly ask management about cases of respiratory illness, such as asthma and bronchitis, but that they were not alerted to the issue. An investigation by the California Department of Public Health found that Amazon failed to notify the state about 10 asthma-related COVID cases in 2013 and 2014.
The complaints from Amazon workers were detailed in a 65-page report, which alleged that workers were receiving no information on the safety of the warehouse’s ventilation system, despite it being a major source of the illness at the facility.

The Outcome Of COVID-19 Cases
“Amazon is an amazing company, but their e-commerce business has been giving them a lot of trouble,” said Alameda County Public Health Officer Dr. Barbara Ferrer, according to The Mercury News. “Part of this agreement is a commitment that Amazon will make sure their warehouses are safe environments for workers,” said Dr. Ferrer.

Both the California Department of Public Health (CDPH) and the City of Oakland monitored Amazon’s warehouses for human resources violations that would increase the risk of worker illness. But according to the Times, Amazon ignored the requests for human resources reviews and surveillance of COVID cases at its warehouses, which were in violation of the Bay Area Air Quality Management District (BAAQMD) regulations.

Conclusion:
The mere discovery that Amazon and other companies are locking workers inside secretive IDAHO-ish factories with high risk for physical and emotional injuries is scary. While the media often focuses on workers’ stories, which are often eye-opening and shocking, we must remember that the purpose of these forced or near-forced IDAHO-like facilities is to shut workers out of other job opportunities. Amazon’s price tag on forced secrecy is high, but it is no surprise that workers struggle to bring attention to it.
